AFTERMATH

Peace-making

The Armistice on November 11th was preceded by those with Germany’s allies which involved Bulgaria, Ottoman Turkey and Austria-Hungary at the time. As the allied armies moved into the defeated lands, Europe entered a strange twilight in which war was over but peace had not yet come. This could only occur when the Allies gathered in Paris under the presiding figure of Woodrow Wilson for a conference that would seek to remake the European and world orders. The Allies had to keep millions of men under arms until the treaty with Germany was signed at Versailles on June 28th, 1919, with other treaties following down to mid-1920.


Social Change

The end of the war brought about profound social changes. By mobilizing the economy, war industries would be created in return. Women struggling to sustain their families also took up the labor in factories and on the land to in the men's absence. Skilled workers who had been called up as soldiers in 1914 returned to produce munitions. They were inevitably resented as “shirkers” by those still at the front. In France, men came from the colonies as soldiers and workers while African-Americans arrived with the US army. For the first time ever, people “of colour” arrived in Europe together.

The Wars After the First World War

The end of the Great War brought relief to millions of people all over the world. Even so, there were still some conflicts that would remain unresolved for years. In 1918, the Bolshevik revolution imploded in counter-revolution and civil war. Fear of Bolshevism swept Europe and America. Eventually the allies intervening in Russia would be unsuccessful in a bid to crush the menace.

As the the Austro-Hungarian and Ottoman empires neared its collapse, the demands for national sovereignity would explode into violence and border wars as the successor nation-states fought to establish their existence on the ground. Poland, Lithuania, Finland and Yugoslavia, among others, experienced violent birth pangs, as did independent Ireland.

Revolution and counter-revolution swept Germany, Austria and Hungary, with dissatisfied nationalists and military officers forming paramilitary units to crush the radical workers’ movements and fight the border wars. Something similar happened in Italy. Though a victor, it was felt by many to have lost out in the peace. Meanwhile, in the Ottoman Empire, which the Allies had carved up into spheres of influence, the charismatic leader, Kemal Attaturk, fought a war to establish an independent Turkey. The violence brought about these conflicts would continue to persist until 1923.

Remembrance

When the soldiers returned home, they were often greeted, even in defated Germany, by local ceremonies. Major victory parades were held in Paris and London in July 1919 to celebrate the Treaty of Versailles. Other allied capitals followed suit, but there could be no equivalent in the defeated powers.
